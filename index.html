Simons Collaboration on Dark Matter Dynamics

Building the theoretical framework to translate concrete particle-physics models of short-range forces into self-consistent, falsifiable predictions on galactic and sub-galactic scales — turning the coming flood of observations into tests of fundamental interactions.

Director: Manoj Kaplinghat  ·  Deputy Director: Mariangela Lisanti

Overview

Dark matter (DM) makes up about 85% of the matter in the Universe, yet we still do not know whether its particles interact through anything other than gravity.

The standard Cold Dark Matter paradigm assumes DM is cold and collisionless. Current data are consistent with DM being effectively cold for galaxy formation, but the collisionless hypothesis remains poorly tested on galactic and sub-galactic scales. Data from the Vera C. Rubin Observatory, Euclid, JWST, and the Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope now have the statistical power and spatial resolution to detect signatures of collisional dark matter.

Existing theoretical frameworks cannot yet predict these signatures self-consistently across mass scales and time, nor cleanly separate astrophysical processes from non-gravitational DM dynamics. Our Collaboration is built to remove that limitation.

Why search for a short-range force between DM particles?

Assuming DM interacts only gravitationally is a minimal working hypothesis, not a data-driven conclusion. In fact, no other particle in the Standard Model behaves this way. Short-range forces — the strong nuclear force, forces between atoms — are familiar and ubiquitous, and arise generically in extensions of the Standard Model such as hidden-sector gauge theories and asymmetric or composite dark matter.

Such self-interactions leave large cosmic scales — the Cosmic Microwave Background and linear structure formation — essentially untouched. Their effects concentrate on smaller scales, reshaping the internal structure of galaxies and their population statistics. A discovery would reveal a new fundamental force beyond the four we know today.

How a new force reshapes galaxies

A short-range force allows elastic and inelastic collisions between DM particles. These microscopic interactions drive macroscopic heat flow through the DM halo around each galaxy. As internal energy is redistributed, the halo's density — and the gravitational potential felt by the central galaxy — changes.

In isolated halos, self-interactions first push heat outward, forming shallow "cored" density profiles; eventually the flow reverses and the core undergoes runaway gravothermal collapse, a uniquely SIDM phenomenon that may seed dense dark substructures and even supermassive black holes. When a small halo falls into a larger host, additional effects appear: evaporation mass loss, drag forces, and altered orbits.

Research program

The Collaboration uses a coordinated, multi-tier modeling infrastructure — state-of-the-art N-body and hydrodynamical simulations ((MP-)GADGET, AREPO, ChaNGa, GIZMO), calibrated semi-analytic models, and machine learning — to identify observables that break the degeneracy between DM dynamics and baryonic feedback across five environments.

  • Aim 1 · Low velocity

    Low-mass galaxies

    Constraining low-velocity DM collisions with dwarf galaxies — clean, baryon-poor laboratories for DM physics and rotation-curve diversity.

  • Aim 2 · Low / mid velocity

    Milky Way analogs

    Constraining DM collisions through satellite populations, tidal stripping, and evaporation around Milky-Way-like hosts.

  • Aim 3 · Low / mid velocity

    Lensing galaxies

    Constraining DM collisions in strong-lens host galaxies via flux-ratio anomalies and gravitational imaging perturbers.

  • Aim 4 · Mid / high velocity

    Galaxy clusters

    Constraining mid/high-velocity DM collisions with cluster zoom simulations and satellite-galaxy and lensing-arc statistics.

  • Aim 5 · Low / mid velocity

    High redshifts

    Constraining DM collisions in the early Universe, including SIDM seeding of supermassive black holes to compare with JWST "Little Red Dots."

Organization & activities

The program is carried out by 10 founding Principal Investigators together with postdoctoral researchers and graduate students distributed across Princeton, UC San Diego, UC Riverside, and the University of Virginia. It is led by Director Manoj Kaplinghat and Deputy Director Mariangela Lisanti.
