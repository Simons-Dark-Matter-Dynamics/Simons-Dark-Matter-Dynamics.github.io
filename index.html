Dark-matter halo — a collisional particle exchanging momentum

01 · Overview

85% of the matter in the Universe is dark — and we still don't know if it feels anything but gravity.

The standard Cold Dark Matter paradigm assumes dark matter is cold and collisionless. Current data are consistent with it being effectively cold for galaxy formation, but the collisionless hypothesis remains poorly tested on galactic and sub-galactic scales.

Data from the Vera C. Rubin Observatory, Euclid, JWST, and the Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope now have the statistical power and spatial resolution to detect the signatures of collisional dark matter — if we know what to look for.

Existing frameworks can't yet predict those signatures self-consistently across mass and time, nor separate astrophysics from non-gravitational dark-matter dynamics. This collaboration is built to remove that limitation.

Heat flows out (core), then reverses (gravothermal collapse)

02 · The Physics

A short-range force would reshape galaxies from the inside out.

Assuming dark matter interacts only gravitationally is a minimal working hypothesis, not a data-driven conclusion — no other particle in the Standard Model behaves that way. Short-range forces are familiar and ubiquitous, and arise generically in extensions of the Standard Model. Such self-interactions leave the cosmic microwave background and large-scale structure essentially untouched, concentrating their effects on smaller scales.

A short-range force lets dark-matter particles collide, driving heat through the halo around each galaxy. Early on, heat flows outward and carves shallow cored density profiles; eventually the flow reverses and the core undergoes runaway gravothermal collapse — a uniquely self-interacting phenomenon that may seed dense dark substructures and even supermassive black holes. A discovery would reveal a fifth fundamental force.

03 · Research

Five environments, one axis: collision velocity.

We coordinate state-of-the-art N-body and hydrodynamical simulations ((MP-)GADGET, AREPO, ChaNGa, GIZMO), calibrated semi-analytic models, and machine learning to find observables that break the degeneracy between dark-matter dynamics and baryonic feedback — from the slowest collisions in dwarf galaxies to the fastest in galaxy clusters.

Aim 01Low velocity

Low-mass galaxies

Dwarf galaxies are the smallest, most baryon-poor halos known — clean laboratories for constraining low-velocity dark-matter collisions and explaining rotation-curve diversity.

Aim 02Low / mid velocity

Milky Way analogs

Satellite populations around Milky-Way-like hosts encode tidal stripping, evaporation, and drag — disentangling dark-matter physics from baryonic feedback.

Aim 03Low / mid velocity

Lensing galaxies

Strong-lens host galaxies turn substructure into a measurable signal through flux-ratio anomalies and gravitational imaging perturbers.

Aim 04Mid / high velocity

Galaxy clusters

The fastest collisions occur in clusters: zoom simulations plus satellite-galaxy and lensing-arc statistics probe the high-velocity regime.

Aim 05Low / mid velocity · high redshift

The early Universe

At high redshift, gravothermal seeding of supermassive black holes can be confronted with JWST "Little Red Dots" and luminous quasars.
